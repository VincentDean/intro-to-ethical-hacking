01
What Is OSINT?
Intelligence gathered entirely from public sources

OSINT — Open Source Intelligence — is the practice of collecting and analyzing information from publicly available sources. No unauthorized access required. No systems touched without permission. Just knowing where to look and how to connect the dots.

Governments, corporations, journalists, private investigators, and security researchers all rely on OSINT. For ethical hackers, it's the starting point of every engagement — understand your target before you ever touch their systems.

In the Downtown Donuts scenario

Before your team attempts any technical exploit, you gather intelligence. Who runs the network? What domains do they own? What services are exposed? What does their digital footprint reveal? That's OSINT.

No footprint
Passive OSINT leaves no trace — the target doesn't know you're looking.
Widely used
Law enforcement, journalists, HR departments, and red teams all use OSINT daily.
First phase
In any pentest, recon comes before scanning, exploitation, or anything else.
Legal — if passive
Using public data is legal. Accessing private systems without permission is not.
02
Passive vs. Active Reconnaissance
Understanding the line between observation and interaction
Passive Recon
Pure OSINT — no contact with target
You gather intelligence from third-party sources — search engines, public records, social media, DNS registries. The target has no way of knowing you're looking. This is where OSINT lives.
Active Recon
Direct interaction — leaves traces
You directly interact with the target system — running nmap scans, pinging hosts, probing services. Faster and more detailed, but it leaves traces and requires explicit authorization.
Authorization required for active recon

Running nmap or any probe against systems you don't own — without written permission — is illegal under the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act. In this course, active recon is only performed on AuraLab targets.

03
Key Vocabulary
Terms you need before your first OSINT operation
OSINT
Open Source Intelligence — intelligence gathered from publicly available sources.
Passive Recon
Gathering info without interacting with the target. No trace left behind.
Active Recon
Directly probing target systems. Leaves traces. Requires authorization.
Footprint
The total amount of publicly available information about a target.
Google Dorking
Using advanced Google search operators to surface sensitive or specific data.
WHOIS
A public database of domain registration records — owner, registrar, dates.
DNS Enumeration
Querying DNS records to map a target's infrastructure (A, MX, TXT, NS records).
Metadata
Hidden data embedded in files — can reveal author, GPS location, software used.
Shodan
A search engine for internet-connected devices — reveals open ports and exposed services.
SOCMINT
Social Media Intelligence — using social platforms as OSINT data sources.
04
What You Can Find
Two categories of OSINT intelligence targets
Technical Targets
  • Domain names and subdomains
  • IP address ranges
  • Exposed ports and services (Shodan)
  • SSL certificates and metadata
  • Email addresses
  • Tech stack and software versions
Human Targets
  • Employee names and roles (LinkedIn)
  • Social media profiles and activity
  • Public records and business filings
  • Document metadata (author, location)
  • Username patterns across platforms
  • Photos and embedded geolocation data
Remember: public ≠ fair game

Collecting OSINT on real individuals without authorization can violate privacy laws and terms of service. In this course, all techniques are practiced on fictional targets (Downtown Donuts) or on yourself.

05
Tools Overview
The OSINT toolkit — what each tool does and when to use it
Tool Category What It Does
theHarvester Email / Subdomain harvesting Pulls emails, subdomains, and hostnames from public sources. Built into Kali Linux.
whois Domain registration Queries registration records for a domain — owner, registrar, expiration date.
Shodan Device search engine Finds internet-connected devices by port, service, location, or banner — no touching the target.
Google Dorking Search operator techniques Uses operators like site:, filetype:, intitle: to surface sensitive indexed data.
Maltego CE Visual link analysis Maps relationships between domains, IPs, people, and organizations as a visual graph.
Recon-ng Recon framework Modular Python-based framework for web reconnaissance — similar to Metasploit for OSINT.
ExifTool Metadata extraction Extracts metadata from images, PDFs, and documents — can reveal GPS, author, device info.
Wayback Machine Archived web pages Retrieves old versions of websites — useful for finding removed content or prior configurations.
Kali Linux has most of these pre-installed

theHarvester, Recon-ng, ExifTool, and Maltego are all available on your Kali USB. Shodan and Google Dorking work from any browser.

ACT
Research Activity — OSINT in the Real World
Investigate how OSINT is used professionally and ethically
Overview

OSINT isn't just a hacker technique — it's used by journalists, law enforcement, human rights investigators, and corporate security teams every day. Your job is to research one real-world OSINT use case and present your findings to the class.

Step 1
Choose a Use Case

Select one of the following OSINT application areas to research:

  • Investigative journalism — how reporters use OSINT to verify stories and locate sources
  • Law enforcement — how agencies use open-source data in criminal investigations
  • Human rights investigations — organizations like Bellingcat using OSINT to document atrocities
  • Corporate threat intelligence — security teams monitoring for brand exposure and leaked credentials
  • Missing persons — volunteer OSINT communities helping locate missing individuals
Step 2
Research Your Use Case

Find at least two sources (article, video, case study, or official documentation) and answer the following:

  • Who uses OSINT in this field, and what is their goal?
  • What specific tools or techniques are commonly used?
  • Describe one real case or example from your research
  • What are the ethical considerations or risks in this use case?
  • How does this use case connect to what we've learned about passive vs. active recon?
Step 3
Present Your Findings
  • Prepare a 3–5 minute verbal summary to share with the class — no slides required
  • Be ready to answer: "What surprised you most about this use case?"
  • Submit your sources and written notes to your shared drive folder before presenting
Deliverable

Written notes (bullet points are fine) + two cited sources, saved to your shared drive folder. Verbal presentation to the class.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Treating "public" as "authorized" — it isn't  ·  Jumping to active recon before exhausting passive sources  ·  Forgetting to document your sources and methodology  ·  Running theHarvester or Shodan queries on real targets without permission
