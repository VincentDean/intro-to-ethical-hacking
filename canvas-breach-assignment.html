Canvas LMS Breach

Investigate a real security incident, classify the attack using industry frameworks, and brief your team like a professional analyst.

Overview
Mission Brief

You are a junior security analyst hired to brief your team on the Canvas breach. Before your organization adopts Canvas district-wide, leadership wants to understand the risk. Your deliverable is the briefing that shapes that decision.

Canvas, one of the most widely used Learning Management Systems in K-12 and higher education, recently experienced a significant security incident. Your job is to investigate what happened, break it down using the frameworks and vocabulary from this course, and present your findings in a format of your choosing.

This is a self-directed assignment. You set your pace, choose your deliverable, and own your research process. All formats are graded on the same rubric.

Learning Objectives
  • Identify the type of vulnerability or attack vector involved in the Canvas breach
  • Classify the incident using a recognized framework — CVE, OWASP Top 10, or MITRE ATT&CK
  • Evaluate the real-world impact on students, educators, and institutions
  • Propose at least two technically grounded remediation strategies
  • Communicate findings clearly for a technical or semi-technical audience
Investigation Steps
01
Research the Breach

Using credible sources — security blogs, CVE databases, news outlets, and Instructure's official statements — gather information to answer all of the following:

  • What happened? Who was affected, when, and how was it discovered?
  • What vulnerability or technique was exploited? (e.g., broken access control, API misconfiguration, credential stuffing)
  • What data or systems were exposed?
  • How did Canvas / Instructure respond?
02
Classify the Attack

Map the breach to at least one recognized framework. Don't just name it — explain why it fits this specific incident.

  • OWASP Top 10 — which category applies and why?
  • MITRE ATT&CK — which tactic or technique matches the attacker behavior?
  • CVE entry — if one exists, cite it. If not, document that gap and explain what a CVE entry should contain based on what you found.
03
Analyze the Impact — All Three Layers

Strong security analysis considers every layer of impact, not just the technical damage.

  • Technical: What systems, APIs, or data were compromised?
  • Institutional: How does this affect schools or districts using Canvas?
  • Human: What are the privacy implications for students and staff?
04
Recommend Remediations

Propose at least two specific remediations. These must be technically grounded and feasible — not generic advice like "use stronger passwords."

Think about both short-term patches and long-term architectural fixes. The best security teams address both.

Ethics

The human layer matters most. Student data — grades, communications, and personally identifiable information — is protected under FERPA. Consider what obligations a school district has when its tools are breached, and what students are owed in terms of disclosure and remediation.

Deliverable Menu — Choose One
Security Brief
1–2 page written report structured as a professional incident summary.
Slide Deck
8–10 slides presenting the breach, classification, impact, and remediation.
Threat Model
Annotated diagram mapping the attack path, assets at risk, and mitigations.
Video Explainer
3–5 minute recorded walkthrough — screen share or talking head.
Mock Patch Notes
Write the official remediation release as if you were Canvas's security team.
Tip

All five formats are graded on the same rubric. Pick the one that plays to your strengths, not the one that looks easiest.

What to Submit
  • Your completed artifact in the chosen format, submitted to Canvas
  • A 1-paragraph reflection — what would you have done differently if you were on Canvas's security team?
  • A source list with at least 3 credible references: CVE database entry, vendor advisory, or reputable security publication
Required

Credible sources matter. Avoid unverified blogs or social media claims. Security analysis is only as strong as the evidence behind it. Cite everything.

Exit Ticket
1
What OWASP Top 10 category best fits this breach, and why?
2
Name one thing Canvas did right in their response and one thing they should have done faster.
3
If this breach happened at our school using Canvas, what is the first action you would take as the IT security lead?
// Post all three answers in your Canvas submission comments before submitting — 1–2 sentences each
