What This Project Is
The Assignment

In this course, you have learned how attackers think — how tools like nmap, Hydra, and Burp Suite work, and why ethical boundaries matter. Now it is time to apply that knowledge to the real world.

You will research a real, documented cybersecurity incident and write a three-page analytical paper explaining what happened, why it mattered, and what defenders could have done differently.

Important Note

This is not a summary assignment. You are expected to analyze the incident using what you know about security concepts and professional methodology. Your paper must demonstrate that you understand the attack — not just that you can look it up.

Connection to Class

Every incident on the topic list connects to tools and concepts you have used in this class. As you write, ask yourself: Have we seen this type of attack before? What tool could detect or prevent this? Making those connections is what earns full credit on the Technical Understanding section of the rubric.

Deliverables
  • Three full pages of body text (minimum)
  • MLA format throughout (heading, in-text citations, spacing)
  • Works Cited page as a separate page at the end
  • Minimum 3 credible sources cited correctly
  • File name: LastName_CyberIncident_Research.docx

Paper Requirements
Format
  • MLA format throughout — heading, in-text citations, Works Cited page
  • Times New Roman, 12pt font, double-spaced
  • 1-inch margins on all sides
  • Last name and page number in the upper-right header (e.g., Smith 1)
  • Standard MLA heading block on the first page: Name → Course → School → Date
  • Title centered on the first page — no bold, no special formatting
Length
  • Minimum 3 full pages of body text (not counting the Works Cited page)
  • Works Cited appears on its own separate page at the end
  • A paper that runs short will lose points under Content & Analysis
Sources
  • Minimum 3 credible sources — news articles, government reports, security research, academic papers
  • Wikipedia may be used for background only — it cannot be a primary source
  • AI-generated content may not be cited as a source
  • Every source must appear in both in-text citations AND the Works Cited page
Good Source Examples
  • cisa.gov — U.S. government cybersecurity alerts and advisories
  • krebsonsecurity.com — professional security journalism
  • theregister.com, wired.com — reputable tech journalism
  • Published books on specific incidents (e.g., Countdown to Zero Day, Sandworm)
  • Peer-reviewed academic papers from security conferences (IEEE, USENIX)
Academic Integrity

Copying text from any source without citation is plagiarism. Submitting AI-generated writing as your own is academic dishonesty. Both result in a zero and may lead to further disciplinary action. Your sources should support your analysis — not replace it.

Step 3 of 7
Paper Structure

Your paper does not need section headers. These four areas are a guide for your thinking, not a rigid outline you must follow. Use them to make sure you are covering everything the rubric expects.

1
What Happened

Describe the incident clearly and accurately. Who was attacked? When did it happen? How did the attacker get in? What systems or data were affected? Use specific facts from your sources.

Who was the victim organization?
What was the attacker's goal — data theft, disruption, money?
How long did the attack go undetected?
2
The Technical Breakdown

Explain the security weaknesses that made the attack possible. Identify the attack type. Connect what you learned in class to what actually happened. This is where you demonstrate technical understanding.

What vulnerability or weakness did the attacker exploit?
Is this related to anything we did with nmap, Hydra, or Burp Suite?
What type of attack is this — brute force, social engineering, exploit?
3
Impact and Response

What were the consequences — financial, operational, political, or social? How did the victim organization respond? Was the response effective? What would a professional security team have done differently?

What did the breach actually cost — money, trust, operations?
Did the organization respond quickly and transparently?
Were there legal or regulatory consequences?
4
Lessons Learned

What should organizations take away from this incident? What defensive practices — patching, access control, network segmentation, employee training — could have prevented it? Connect your recommendations to real security principles.

What one change would have stopped this attack?
Does this incident change how you think about any lab we did?
What advice would you give a company's security team today?

Step 5 of 7
MLA Format Guide

MLA (Modern Language Association) format has specific rules for how to format your paper and cite your sources. Below are the most common citation types you will need and examples of each.

In-Text Citations
  • Place citations inside the sentence, before the period: (Author LastName PageNumber).
  • For websites with no page numbers: (Author LastName).
  • If no author is listed, use a shortened version of the title: ("WannaCry Attack").
  • The in-text citation must match a full entry in the Works Cited page.
Works Cited — Website / News Article
Format
Last, First. "Article Title." Website Name, Day Month Year, URL.
Example
Heath, Ryan. "The Colonial Pipeline Hack, Explained." Politico, 10 May 2021, www.politico.com/news/2021/05/10/colonial-pipeline-hack-486534.
Works Cited — Book
Format
Last, First. Book Title: Subtitle If Any. Publisher, Year.
Example
Greenberg, Andy. Sandworm: A New Era of Cyberwar and the Hunt for the Kremlin's Most Dangerous Hackers. Doubleday, 2019.
Works Cited — Government Report
Format
Country, Agency Name. Report Title. Agency Abbreviation, Date, URL.
Example
United States, Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency. Alert AA21-131A: DarkSide Ransomware Best Practices. CISA, 11 May 2021, www.cisa.gov/uscert/ncas/alerts/aa21-131a.
Works Cited — Blog / Security Research
Example
Krebs, Brian. "What We Know About the Mirai Botnet." KrebsOnSecurity, 17 Oct. 2016, krebsonsecurity.com/2016/10/hacked-cameras-dvrs-powered-todays-massive-internet-outage.
Quick Rules
  • Works Cited entries use a hanging indent — second and later lines are indented 0.5 inches
  • Entries are listed alphabetically by author's last name
  • Months with 5+ letters are abbreviated: Jan., Feb., Mar., Apr., Aug., Sept., Oct., Nov., Dec.
  • Book and website titles are italicized; article titles go in "quotation marks"
