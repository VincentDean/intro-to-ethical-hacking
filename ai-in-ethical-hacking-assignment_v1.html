Self-Directed Assignment · 100 Points

AI in Ethical Hacking

Research how artificial intelligence is changing the offense and defense sides of cybersecurity — then stake out your own informed position.

Due Thu May 21 100 Points 5 Research Areas 4 Days Individual 3+ Sources Required
Overview
Mission Brief

Artificial intelligence is reshaping every layer of cybersecurity. The tools used to detect and stop attacks are now being used to launch them. Security professionals in 2025 don't just need to know how to run tools — they need to understand how AI is changing the whole game, on both sides.

In this assignment you will independently research the history, current state, and future implications of AI in ethical hacking. You will examine how AI is being used in offense (by attackers) and in defense (by defenders), how it is changing the pen tester's job, and where it is all heading.

  • What You Will Turn In
  • Written report — 1,200 words, organized by the five research areas below
  • Cover page following MLA format — Purdue OWL MLA General Format ↗
  • Minimum 3 sources you found yourself, cited in MLA or APA (pick one and stay consistent)
  • One briefing slide — a single slide summarizing your most important finding
  • Two-minute team presentation on Day 4 (Thu, May 21)
  • Research log — a running list of every source you visited, even ones you did not use — for up to 5 bonus points
Learning Objectives
  • Explain how AI-powered tools differ from rule-based security tools, historically and today
  • Identify specific ways AI is being used on defense to detect and respond to threats
  • Identify specific ways AI is being used on offense to carry out more sophisticated attacks
  • Evaluate AI tools being built into professional penetration testing workflows
  • Form and defend an original, evidence-based position on where AI in cybersecurity is heading
Research Areas
01 History — How Did We Get Here? 20 pts

Trace the evolution of AI and automation in security — from early antivirus heuristics to modern large language models.

  • 1.1What is the difference between a rule-based security tool and an AI-powered security tool? Give one example of each.
  • 1.2When did AI and machine learning begin appearing in commercial security products? Name a specific early example and describe what it did.
  • 1.3What problem were early AI security tools trying to solve that traditional tools could not handle?
  • 1.4How has the arrival of generative AI — tools like ChatGPT and GitHub Copilot — changed the conversation compared to earlier machine learning tools?
Research Tip

Search "history of machine learning in cybersecurity" and "intrusion detection systems AI evolution." DARPA's anomaly detection research in the 1990s is a strong starting point.

02 Defense — AI as a Protective Tool 20 pts

Defenders are using AI to detect and respond to threats faster than any human team could alone. Explore how AI is being applied to defense in real organizations today.

  • 2.1What is a SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) system? How does AI improve it compared to older, rule-only systems?
  • 2.2What is behavioral analysis in security monitoring? How does AI make it possible at scale?
  • 2.3Find and describe one real AI-powered security product in use today — such as Darktrace, CrowdStrike Falcon, or Microsoft Sentinel. What does it claim to do?
  • 2.4What are two limitations of relying too heavily on AI for defense? Consider false positives, blind spots, cost, and bias in your answer.
Career Note

Security Operations Center analysts are increasingly expected to work alongside AI tools. Understanding how these systems work — and where they fail — is a real job skill for 2025 and beyond.

03 Offense — AI as a Weapon 20 pts

The same AI techniques used to protect systems can be turned around and used to attack them. Examine how threat actors — from cybercriminals to nation-states — are using AI to hit harder and move faster.

  • 3.1What is AI-generated phishing? How does it differ from traditional phishing emails, and why is it more dangerous?
  • 3.2What are deepfakes and how have they been used in cyberattacks or social engineering campaigns? Find one documented real-world example.
  • 3.3What is autonomous AI malware? What would make it different from traditional malware in terms of how it spreads and adapts?
  • 3.4How has AI lowered the skill floor for launching attacks? What does this mean for the volume and frequency of attacks going forward?
Framing Reminder

The goal here is to understand threats — not to launch them. Document your findings like a threat intelligence analyst writing a brief for their organization. Describe what attackers do; do not explain how to replicate it.

04 Tools — AI in the Pen Tester's Workflow 20 pts

AI is now built directly into the ethical hacking workflow. Pen testers use it to speed up reconnaissance, analyze scan results, write reports, and generate custom scripts for authorized tests.

  • 4.1How are ethical hackers using large language models in penetration testing? Give two specific examples, such as writing scripts or summarizing scan output.
  • 4.2What is AI-assisted vulnerability scanning? How does it differ from running nmap on its own?
  • 4.3Research one AI tool built for security professionals — such as Pentest Copilot, HackerGPT, or AI-assisted features inside Burp Suite. What does it do and what are its stated safeguards against misuse?
  • 4.4Even when AI handles part of the work, what responsibility do you still carry? Does AI change who is legally and ethically accountable for the results of a penetration test?
AuraLab Connection

Think about the tools you have used this semester — nmap, Hydra, Burp Suite. How would an AI layer change how you use each one? What would it still require you to understand on your own?

05 Your Take — The Big Picture 20 pts

After completing your research, form and defend your own position. There are no single correct answers here, but your argument must be grounded in evidence from what you found.

  • 5.1Is AI currently more useful to offense or to defense? Defend your position with evidence from your research.
  • 5.2Some argue AI will eventually make human ethical hackers unnecessary. Others argue that human judgment will always be essential. Where do you stand, and why?
  • 5.3Identify one ethical concern about AI in cybersecurity that has not been resolved. This could be about privacy, bias in detection systems, autonomous offensive tools, access controls, or anything else you encountered in your research.
  • 5.4If you were advising Downtown Donuts on whether to invest in AI security tools today, what would you tell them? Be specific about what they should do and what they should be cautious about.
Writing Quality

Part 5 is where you demonstrate professional judgment, not just recall. Write in full paragraphs, take a clear position, and back it up with evidence from your research. This section should read like an analyst's recommendation, not a list of observations.

Ethics

AI does not remove ethical responsibility — it redistributes it. When AI makes a recommendation, a human still has to decide whether to act on it. As a practitioner, understanding what AI can and cannot be trusted to do is part of your professional obligation. Write about this in your report with that framing in mind.

Starter Sources

These are starting points only. You must find and cite at least 3 additional sources beyond this list. All sources must be cited in your report.

  • NIST AI 100-2 — Adversarial Machine Learning: A Taxonomy and Terminology — official U.S. government framework for understanding AI threats in security systems
  • MIT Technology Review — search "AI cybersecurity arms race" — explains the dual-use nature of AI in both offense and defense
  • Darktrace Blog — "How AI Changes Threat Detection" — practical defense perspective from a major industry vendor
  • SANS Institute — search "AI-augmented penetration testing" — deep-dive reading list on AI in pen testing workflows
  • CyberWire Daily Podcast — any recent episode covering AI threats — up-to-date news from working security professionals
  • Wired — search "AI-powered phishing" — accessible breakdown of AI-generated social engineering
  • DEF CON / Black Hat on YouTube — search "AI hacking" — real researcher presentations; among the most credible primary sources available
Required

Credible sources matter. Avoid unverified blogs or social media posts. Security analysis is only as strong as the evidence behind it. Cite everything. If you cannot find a credible source for a claim, do not include the claim.
