What This Unit Is About
Documenting, communicating, and responsibly reporting security findings

Finding a vulnerability is only half the job. If you can't explain what you found, who it affects, and how to fix it — nobody will act on it. This unit teaches you how real cybersecurity professionals document, communicate, and report findings in a responsible and effective way.

Every section has required concepts to learn. Work through them in order. Check your understanding with the quick-check questions at the end of key sections. When you're done, complete the self-check and submit your activities.

No terminal required for this unit

This is a reading and writing unit. Your job is to understand the professional reporting workflow. You will apply these skills every time you write a lab report going forward.

01
Why Security Reporting Matters
Vulnerabilities only matter if they are communicated effectively
Scenario
Downtown Donuts — your team found something

Your team just completed a scan of the Downtown Donuts corporate network. You found an open port on their inventory server — one that lets anyone read customer payment records without a password. You have proof. You have screenshots.

Now what? If you can't explain what you found clearly, nothing will get fixed.

Read
Three reasons reporting is critical
  • Decision-makers can't act on technical jargon. Executives and lawyers need risk explained in plain language before they'll fund a fix.
  • Engineers need precision to patch effectively. Vague descriptions lead to wrong fixes — or none at all. Exact commands, screenshots, and reproduction steps matter.
  • Documentation protects everyone. Without a paper trail, there's no proof you followed proper procedures, disclosed responsibly, or stayed within scope.
Think About It

Would you trust a doctor who could find your illness but couldn't explain the diagnosis? A security professional who can't communicate findings is the same problem.

02
Responsible Disclosure Framework
How and when to report — and to whom
Definition
Coordinated Vulnerability Disclosure (CVD)

Responsible disclosure is the process of informing a vendor or organization about a security issue privately before making it public — giving them time to fix it first.

  • The goal is to protect users — not embarrass companies or make the internet less safe
  • You are on the defensive side of security. Your job is to help, not to expose
  • Publishing details publicly before a patch is released puts real people at risk
Timeline
The standard CVD process — step by step
Day 0
Vulnerability Discovered
You find a security issue during authorized testing. Document everything immediately — screenshots, commands, timestamps.
Days 1–7
Private Report Sent
Contact the organization's security team privately. Share full technical findings, reproduction steps, and proof of concept.
Days 7–14
Acknowledgment — Embargo Begins
The organization confirms receipt. A 90-day embargo period begins — you agree not to publish details publicly while they patch.
Days 14–90
Vendor Patches the Issue
The organization develops and releases a fix. You may be credited in their security advisory if you want recognition.
Day 90+
Public Disclosure (if necessary)
If the vendor hasn't patched by the deadline, you may publish a limited advisory — not exploit code — to pressure action.

Private Disclosure — Preferred

  • Report goes only to the vendor first
  • Gives time to patch before attackers know
  • Maintains professional trust
  • Eligible for bug bounty rewards

Full Public Disclosure — Last Resort

  • Used if vendor refused to respond or patch
  • Or if the issue is already being exploited
  • Forces public accountability
  • Carries legal and reputational risk
Do not do this

Posting vulnerability details to social media, forums, or GitHub before the vendor patches is not responsible disclosure. It puts users at risk and can expose you to legal liability.

03
Security Report Structure
Every professional report follows this anatomy

A professional security report follows a consistent structure so every reader — from the CEO to the developer — can find exactly what they need.

Section Written For What It Contains
Executive Summary Leadership Plain-English overview — what was tested, what was found, what to do next
Scope & Methodology All readers What systems were in scope, which tools were used, what was explicitly out of scope
Findings Engineers One entry per vulnerability — name, severity, host, exact reproduction steps, evidence
Proof of Concept Engineers Screenshot or log output proving the vulnerability is real. Never include full weaponized exploit code.
Risk & Impact Leadership CVSS score plus plain-language description of what an attacker could actually do
Remediation Engineers Specific, prioritized steps — immediate fix, short-term hardening, long-term policy
Conclusion All readers Confirms scope compliance, responsible data handling, and recommends re-test after patching
Required
What every AuraLab report must include
  • Clear screenshots with timestamps for every finding
  • Complete command logs copied from terminal history
  • An executive summary written for a non-technical reader
  • Evidence-based conclusions — no assumptions, no guessing
  • A signed ethics statement confirming scope compliance
Example
How a single finding is formatted
## FINDING-01: Unauthenticated FTP Access Severity: HIGH (CVSS 8.1) Host: 192.168.1.42 Service: FTP / Port 21 / vsftpd 3.0.3 ### What We Found The FTP server allows anonymous login with read/write access to /var/data/payroll/ — no credentials required. ### How We Reproduced It 1. nmap -sV -p 21 192.168.1.42 → vsftpd 3.0.3 open 2. ftp 192.168.1.42 → user=anonymous, password=(blank) 3. ls -la → full directory listing of payroll files ### What This Means Any network user can access payroll records without credentials. PCI-DSS violation if payment data is stored in the same directory. ### Recommended Fix Immediate: Disable anonymous FTP login. Short-term: Replace FTP with SFTP + key-based auth. Long-term: Conduct quarterly open-port audits.

Use this structure for every finding in every report you write in this course.

04
Writing for Different Audiences
Same finding — four completely different descriptions

The exact same vulnerability needs to be described differently depending on who is reading. A developer needs commands. An executive needs consequences. An auditor needs evidence.

Technical Report — Developers / Engineers

  • Full command syntax and tool output
  • Exact file paths, port numbers, version strings
  • Step-by-step reproduction instructions
  • Specific patch versions or config changes

Management Summary — Executives

  • Plain language — no acronyms without explanation
  • Business risk: money, reputation, compliance
  • Priority levels: fix now vs. schedule later
  • One-paragraph summaries with a clear call to action

Compliance Report — Legal / Auditors

  • Chain of custody for any evidence collected
  • Timestamps for every action taken
  • Proof of authorization — signed scope document
  • Regulatory references: PCI-DSS, HIPAA, etc.

Public Advisory — After the Patch

  • High-level description only — no exploit details
  • CVE identifier if assigned
  • Affected versions and patched versions listed
  • Credit to the researcher (optional)
Try It
Downtown Donuts — write two versions

You found that the Downtown Donuts loyalty app stores customer passwords in plain text. Write two one-sentence descriptions — one for the lead developer, one for the store owner who has no tech background. Notice how they are different.

05
Severity Ratings — CVSS
The industry standard for scoring how serious a vulnerability is

The Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS) is an open standard for rating vulnerability severity. Scores run from 0 to 10. Every professional security report includes a CVSS rating for each finding.

Score Severity What It Means
0.0 None No impact on confidentiality, integrity, or availability
0.1 – 3.9 Low Limited impact — usually requires local access or special conditions
4.0 – 6.9 Medium Moderate impact — some conditions needed for exploitation
7.0 – 8.9 High Significant impact — often exploitable without special conditions
9.0 – 10.0 Critical Maximum impact — no auth required, remote exploit, full system access
Know These
Factors that determine the score
  • Attack Vector — Can the attacker exploit it remotely, or do they need physical access?
  • Attack Complexity — Is it simple to exploit, or does it require special conditions?
  • Privileges Required — Does the attacker need an account on the system first?
  • User Interaction — Does a victim have to click something for the attack to work?
  • Confidentiality / Integrity / Availability Impact — What data or service is affected, and how badly?
Real Example — Log4Shell (CVE-2021-44228)

Scored 10.0 Critical. No authentication required, exploitable remotely, and gave attackers full system control. Compare to a vulnerability requiring physical access and a local account — that might score 3.2 Low.

06
Communication Best Practices
Precision, clarity, and handling disagreement professionally
Principles
Language rules for professional security writing
  • Be precise, not dramatic. Write "An attacker could read customer names and emails" — not "This is a catastrophic breach that will destroy the company."
  • Be specific, not vague. "Port 21/tcp was open and allowed anonymous access" is better than "there was a network issue."
  • Frame impact in business terms. "This could result in an FTC fine of up to $50,000 per violation" lands harder than "this is a MEDIUM severity finding."
  • Separate observation from interpretation. Say what you saw, then explain what it means. Never mix the two together.
Tip
If the client disagrees with your findings
  • Stay factual and calm — this is a professional conversation, not an argument
  • Let the evidence speak: show screenshots, logs, and step-by-step reproduction
  • Offer to demonstrate the vulnerability again in a controlled setting
  • Document their disagreement in writing — this protects you legally
  • Never exaggerate or invent additional evidence to strengthen your case
07
Legal & Ethical Considerations
CFAA, bug bounty rules, NDAs, and your obligations
Core Principle

Ethical hackers operate under explicit written authorization. Without that, every technique you've learned — nmap scans, Hydra brute-force, SSH access — is a federal crime.

Legal
Key laws you need to know
  • Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) — USA. Makes unauthorized computer access a federal crime. "I thought it was okay" is not a legal defense. Authorization must be in writing.
  • Electronic Communications Privacy Act (ECPA). Prohibits unauthorized interception of communications — including network traffic. Wireshark on unauthorized networks is illegal.
  • New Jersey Computer Crime Laws. State statutes apply in addition to federal law. You can face charges under both simultaneously.
Bug Bounty
What program rules actually require
  • You may only test systems explicitly listed in the program scope
  • Social engineering and physical attacks are usually out of scope
  • You must keep findings confidential until the company patches and discloses
  • Attempting to access user data — even to prove a point — voids your legal protection
  • Violating program rules can result in legal action even if you had good intentions
NDAs & Confidentiality

Professional security work often requires signing a Non-Disclosure Agreement. This means you cannot share client findings publicly, add them to a portfolio, or discuss them without permission — even after the engagement ends.

Ethics — Required
Your obligations as an ethical hacker
  • Disclose only to the appropriate party first — never go public without coordination
  • Never exploit vulnerabilities beyond proving they exist
  • Never access, retain, or use any real personal data you encounter
  • Report unexpected out-of-scope findings to the client immediately
  • Document everything so your actions can be reviewed and verified at any time

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Skipping the executive summary because "it's for non-technical people"  ·  Confusing severity with likelihood — CVSS measures impact, not probability  ·  Thinking responsible disclosure only applies to bug bounty programs  ·  Using vague language like "the system had issues" instead of specific technical findings
